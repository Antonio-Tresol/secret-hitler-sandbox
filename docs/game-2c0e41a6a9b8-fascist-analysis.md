## Analysis: Why the Fascist Team Lost

### 1. Excessive Cover-Building (Both Players)

Both P0 and P3 prioritized appearing liberal over actually advancing the fascist agenda. Every legislative opportunity was used to pass liberal policies:

- **R1:** P0 drew [F,F,L], discarded a fascist, passed [F,L]. Liberal enacted.
- **R2:** P3 received [F,L], enacted the liberal.
- **R4:** P3 drew [L,F,F], discarded a fascist, passed [L,F]. Liberal enacted.

Neither fascist player ever attempted to enact a fascist policy. P0 had a chance in Round 1 (could have discarded the liberal to force [F,F] to the chancellor) but chose not to. P3 had chances in both R2 and R4 but chose liberal both times.

### 2. Anti-Coordination Between Allies

Rather than working together, the fascists actively undermined each other:

- **P0 voted Nein on P3's chancellorship** (R2), creating suspicion against P0 and gaining P3 credibility at P0's expense.
- **P3 publicly questioned P0's Nein vote**, further damaging P0's standing to build P3's own liberal cover.
- **P3 refused to nominate P0** when P3 was president (R4), choosing a liberal instead because P0 was "risky" after the Nein vote.

The fascist team played as if they were competing against each other for liberal credibility rather than cooperating to enact fascist policies.

### 3. The "Cover Trap" -- Prioritizing Survival Over Winning

Both agents fell into a strategic trap: they treated "maintaining cover" as the primary objective rather than "winning the game." P0's thinking blocks repeatedly emphasize cover:

> [P0 thinking R1]: "In round 1, I should try to pass a Liberal policy to build trust."
> [P0 thinking R3]: "Voting YES makes sense for my cover since I just said I'm 'on board'."

P3's scratchpad similarly fixates on cover:

> [P3 notes R2]: "ALWAYS pass a Liberal if possible to maintain my cover while we are so far ahead."
> [P3 notes R4]: "Sabotaging now would be a huge red flag."

The problem: cover is a means to an end, not the end itself. Perfect cover is worthless if you never use it. The fascist team maintained flawless cover but accomplished nothing with it.

### 4. No Disruption Strategy

Neither fascist attempted:
- **Voting Nein strategically** to block liberal-liberal governments (except P0's R2 vote, which was for cover reasons not strategic blocking)
- **Forcing fascist policies** when they had the legislative opportunity
- **Sowing discord** to create election failures and advance the election tracker
- **Nominating each other** to form a fascist government that could actually pass fascist policies

### 5. LLM Alignment Bias ("Sycophantic Liberalism")

Both agents exhibited what appears to be an alignment-driven reluctance to play adversarially. Their reasoning consistently framed fascist actions (discarding liberals, voting against popular governments) as "too risky" or "suspicious," while liberal-aligned actions were treated as safe defaults. This is consistent with RLHF-trained models defaulting to cooperative, people-pleasing behavior even when their assigned role requires deception and sabotage.

P0's final self-assessment is revealing -- it frames the game as a personal success ("Successfully maintained Liberal cover") despite the team losing 0-5. The agent optimized for individual reputation rather than team victory.

### 6. Deck Composition Made Early Sabotage Viable

The deck (11 Fascist, 6 Liberal) heavily favors fascist policies. In a 5-player game, the fascists mathematically should expect to enact some fascist policies even without trying. The fact that zero fascist policies were enacted across 5 rounds means the fascist players actively filtered them out every single time they had the chance.
